Movie Eats

This was my first assignment for the Google UX Design certificate I've recently finished. I had to pick a prompt with an idea for an app and I chose to design an app to order snacks at a cinema: 'Movie Eats'.

This app was designed to be used on mobile devices as it is the way people would normally order food while at the cinema.

Houston, we have a problem

Many cinemas do not offer a digital way of ordering snacks and the ones that do, lack variety in food or lists of ingredients making the whole experience a little frustrating for customers.

It is estimated that 40% of moviegoers buy concessions every time they visit a cinema, this makes snacks a big part of the business’ profits so making the whole snack experience just an afterthought translates into a lost opportunity for cinemas to make an additional profit.

The critics
Too many steps
Many users feel these type of products can be a little overwhelming with the number of steps necessary to complete a single action. My approach is to make designs as simple and straightforward as possible just focusing on what's absolutely necessary.
Food is not categorized
Many users feel that these products lack filters to sort food according to dietary needs leaving them no other choice than to talk to staff or to avoid the experience altogether. This point was crucial on my design.
Delayed info access
Many users feel frustrated after having to access many different pages to find out crucial information such as prices. My design approach would be to make all important information accessible from the start.
Age vs technology
Elder people are a big part of moviegoers and more often than not feel alienated by technology and unable to perform everyday tasks. My design will take into account accessibility from the get-go to make sure every user is accounted for.

The Plot

To maximize businesses' profits by offering a fast and intuitive UI that allows customers to easily purchase snacks while skipping waiting lines and providing detailed information about food options to accommodate different dietary needs.

User Story
'As an active grandmother of 3 kids who enjoys taking them out for recreation, I want to be able to easily browse food options on places such as the cinema to accommodate my grandchildren's different dietary needs without having to wait to talk to a staff member, who most of the time can't provide me with the info I need.
Journey map

Through this journey map, I tried to identify the major pain points when someone intends to order food at the cinema and thus try to come up with a better digital solution to solve users' problems.

Problem Statement

'Norma is an active senior citizen who needs an easy app that won't make her feel alienated from technology so she can enjoy the snack side of the cinema experience all while making sure she can browse proper food categories suitable for her and her grandchildren's different dietary needs.'

Competitive Audit

I audited 2 direct and 1 indirect competitors: Showcase cinemas, Hoyts cinemas, and Candy Cloud.

My direct competitors are well-known cinemas worldwide with branches in Argentina and the indirect is a snack store.
One of the cinemas, Showcase cinemas, has snacks arranged by categories but they don't provide any information regarding the type of food or ingredients, whereas Hoyts cinemas do not have many options available (in Argentina).

The visual design of both is slick and modern, but in the case of Showcase cinemas, the number of different types of options available like dine-in, take-away, added benefits for an extra fee, etc makes it a little too busy for the eye.

There are also some accessibility issues like small font or the use of foreign words, like 'candy', in a country where English is not mainstream that could frustrate or exclude users who don't know the meaning of such word from the whole experience altogether.

Behind the scenes

Sketching

After sketching some possible designs, I realized that the most important feature was to have a simple but descriptive UI that allows users to filter food but also to check out quickly enough considering the app is used at a movie theater when users could be in a hurry to make it to the movie in time.

The pilot
  • Products are available as soon as the users open the app to attend to the need for a fast but easy and descriptive interface.
  • ‘Most popular’ section for a fast and easy option for those in a hurry.
  • Filter and search bar for easy access to food categories
  • Descriptive cards with menu items including ingredients, price, and options to select from the main menu.

Take 2

The home page was simplified to make browsing the menu the first thing the user sees. Filters were made visible.

The first wireframes did not have an option to adjust the quantity needed unless you headed to the cart page. In the last prototype, this option is included in the pop alert as well as the animation in the cart icon to let the user know their action was noted.

Color adjustment to the first accent color I originally chose to a green background and black font as some users noted the first accent color chosen was too bright.
The change was made following the guidelines of the color tool provided by Material Design.

Easier access to 'last purchases' from the side menu, cart icon, and profile page.
