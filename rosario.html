Rosario es verde

‘Rosario es verde’ is a vegan map of the city of Rosario in Argentina to help the growing vegan/vegetarian community find stores and services suitable for their way of life.

This project was the assignment of the last course of the Google UX Design certificate in which I had to design a user experience for social good.

As part of this community myself, I found myself being asked all the time where I get my groceries, where I could get this and that, are there any plant-based restaurants, etc. I realized that even people who were not vegan themselves had the interest to know and learn.

That's how I came across this idea, to facilitate all this information and allow people to find what they were interested in at their fingertips all while making a good to the planet.

Keeping up with the plants

The vegan community and way of life are growing rapidly and with that the number of places that offer options suitable for vegans.

However, as this market is still evolving, it’s hard for the members of this community and those just trying to find gastronomical alternatives to find all the places offering products suitable for them as well as for small businesses to get the word around.

What to do?

‘Rosario is verde’ is meant to help the people of such city to have a complete and detailed map of the vegan-friendly places in their vicinity as well as to help put small local businesses ‘literally’ and metaphorically on the map.

One place to see them all

I conducted a survey on a vegan community online.

I received a lot of useful feedback which mainly stated that the participants either don't know if there are vegan maps out there or that the ones that exist are not good enough.

Most of them rely on Google or Instagram to find out about vegetarian/vegan places.

The problem with Instagram, for example, is that unless you remember the name of that place you are looking for you can’t really find it.

On the other hand, Google can be too broad and time consuming.

Most of the participants expressed that they would definetely use an app where they can find everytyhing they need and that allows them to filter easily, which was their main struggle.

Meet the users
Isabel's a busy professional, she is vegan, and needs a way to easily explore the vegan options near her so she doesn't end up at the same places all the time.
Juan, on the other hand, is an entrepenueur with a new vegan bakery who needs to reach clients and get the word around in the community.
Keeping it simple

I audited 2 direct and 2 indirect competitors.
The study revealed some good features that I implemented in my design like the ability for users to upload their photos, a link to each place's menu, etc.

It also allow me to see how the filters work and realized that the use of too many colors in a small layout like that of a cell phone can be a little overwhelming.

All in all, it showed that there is plenty of room for improvement to provide a smoother interface.

Now, the fun part

Wireframing

After ideating on paper with exercises like Crazy 8s, I came out with a few ideas for my first wireframes.

My goal was to find a way to make information accessible from the get-go to allow the user to interact with a map and store categories right from the start, all with a clean style.

On the left, they would see the type of stores listed with a corresponding icon to easily identify them on the map and then they can sort those according to dietary needs into vegan, vegetarian, and places with those options in their menus all while seeing the place marked on the map.

Test, test, test...

I tested the low-fidelity prototype created out of the first wireframes with a usability study.

Findings
  • 6 out of 6 participants found they needed feedback from the system regarding whether their actions had been noted.
    The UI needs to give clues about where in the app the user is.
  • 2 out of 6 participants found it frustrating having to scroll all the way up on the store page to hit like.
    Users need a faster way to save places.
  • 1 out of 5 participants found they wanted to hit save on a store they already knew before going to the individual page.
    Users need a faster way to save favorites.
What changed?
  • Dietary needs filters (vegan, vegetarian and vegan options) were reduced to one line only to make more room to see the map.
  • Added the possibility to add a store to favorites from the store-cards view.
  • The score stars were changed to a number to maximize space on the card.
  • When selecting a dietary need, I added the wording into the UI to let the user know exactly what his selection is and not rely solely on the change of color of the dietary bullets on top.
  • The favorites icon on the store page was then set to fixed so the user can store it without going all the way up to save a store in favorites while browsing its page.

Accessibility Considerations
  1. Mobile: Font no smaller than 14p.
  2. Icons are accompanied by text.
  3. Color contrasts meet Material Design standards
  4. Voice search
  5. Currently working on a dark theme.

Website version

After creating the dedicated mobile experience, I started the whole design thinking process again to consider different use cases.

  • What are my users' motivations and goals when using this product on a desktop?
  • How can the companion website support the dedicated mobile app?
The desktop version complements the app with added features like pages with information, online shopping, etc.

Some of the additions include:

  • Landing page with cards of the best-rated places in Rosario.
  • Option to explore the map.
  • Information about veganism and different related articles including how to reduce our impact on global warming.
  • A way to interact with other members of the same community.
  • Access to online stores that support the same values.

Takeaways

This project was challenging since it involved making a lot of information available in the clearest and fastest possible way without making it too crowded or confusing.

As it involved the creation of many different filters to scan information based on common criteria, I learned the importance of having a system to follow that can expand easily but allowing for flexibility at the same time as one needs to be constantly iterating on each phase.

This design was meant for social good and it is a topic that is close to my heart so I really enjoyed it and learned a lot along the way.
