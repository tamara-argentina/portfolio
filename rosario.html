Rosario es verde

‘Rosario es verde’ is a vegan map of the city of Rosario in Argentina to help the growing vegan/vegetarian community find stores and services suitable for their way of life.

The Problem

The vegan community and way of life are growing rapidly and with that the number of places that offer options suitable for vegans.
However, as this market is still evolving, it’s hard for the members of this community and those just trying to find gastronomical alternatives to find all the places offering products suitable for them as well as for small businesses to get the word around in the community.

The Goal

‘Rosario is verde’ is meant to help the people of such city to have a complete and detailed map of the vegan-friendly places in their vicinity as well as to help put small local businesses ‘literally’ and metaphorically on the map.

Understanding the user

User Research

I conducted a survey on a vegan community online.
I received a lot of useful feedback which mainly stated that the participants either don't know if there are vegan maps out there or that the ones that exist are not good enough. Most of them rely on Instagram to find out about places but the problem with Instagram is that unless you remember the name of that place you are looking for you can’t really find it.
Most of the participants stated they would use an app that allow them to filter easily, which was their main struggle.

Persona hypothesis
User Story

'As a busy professional, I want a way to quickly scan for vegan places and vegan options around me so I don't end up getting the same things from the same places all the time.'

User Story

'As an entrepreneur and small business owner, I'd love to have a way to reach the vegan community of my city so as to grow my clientele while making a good impact.'

Problem Statements

'Isabel is a busy professional, vegan since 2020, who needs an app to easily scan for stores, bars, etc with vegan options around her because she always ends up getting the same stuff due to lack of time to do some research.'

'Juan has recently opened a vegan bakery in his hometown of Rosario and needs a way to easily reach the vegan community and spread the word of his vegan local business.'

Competitive Audit

I audited 2 direct and 2 indirect competitors.
The study revealed some good features that I implemented in my design like the ability for users to upload their photos, a link to each place's menu, etc.
It also allow me to see how the filters work and realized that the use of too many colors in a small layout like that of a cell phone can be a little overwhelming.

Starting the design

Wireframes

After ideating on paper with exercises like Crazy 8s, I came out with a few ideas for my first wireframes.

My goal was to find a way to make information accessible from the get-go to allow the user to interact with a map and store categories right from the start, all with a clean style.

On the left, they would see the type of stores listed with a corresponding icon to easily identify them on the map and then they can sort those according to dietary needs into vegan, vegetarian, and places with those options in their menus all while seeing the place marked on the map.

Usability Study

I tested the low-fidelity prototype created out of the first wireframes with a usability study.

Findings
  • 6 out of 6 participants found they needed feedback from the system regarding whether their actions had been noted.
    The UI needs to give clues about where in the app the user is.
  • 2 out of 6 participants found it frustrating having to scroll all the way up on the store page to hit like.
    Users need a faster way to save places.
  • 1 out of 5 participants found they wanted to hit save on a store they already knew before going to the individual page.
    Users need a faster way to save favorites.
Usability Study Impact
  • Added the possibility to add a store to favorites from the store-cards view.
  • The score stars were changed to a number to maximize space on the card.
  • Dietary needs filters were reduced to one line only
  • When selecting a dietary need, I added the wording into the UI to let the user know exactly what his selection is and not rely solely on the change of color of the button on top.
  • The favorites icon on the store page was then set to fixed so the user can store it without going all the way up.

Responsive Design

After creating the dedicated mobile experience, I started the whole design thinking process again to consider different use cases.

  • What are my users' motivations and goals?
  • How can the companion website support the dedicated mobile app?

As this app is meant to be a map to use on the go on a mobile, the desktop version could complement it with added features to make it into a different experience without losing the consistency or coherence of its mobile version.

Some of the additions include:

  • Landing page with cards of the best-rated places in Rosario.
  • Option to explore the map.
  • Information about veganism and different related articles including how to reduce our impact on global warming.
  • A way to interact with other members of the same community.
  • Access to online stores that support the same values.

Takeaways

This project was challenging since it involved making a lot of information available in the clearest and fastest possible way without making it too crowded.
With every project, I learned the importance of having a system to follow but allowing for flexibility at the same time as one needs to be constantly iterating on each phase.

This design was meant for social good and it is a topic that is close to my heart so I really enjoyed it and learned a lot along the way.
